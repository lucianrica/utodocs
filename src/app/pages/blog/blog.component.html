The Quantum Leap

Quantum Leap is an American science-fiction television series, created by Donald P. Bellisario, that originally aired on NBC for five seasons, from March 25, 1989 through May 5, 1993. It stars Scott Bakula as Dr. Sam Beckett, a physicist who leaps through spacetime during experiments in time travel, by temporarily taking the place of other people to correct what he believes were historical mistakes.

He is thrown back in time and, on gaining consciousness, finds that while he physically exists in the past, he appears to everyone else as a person that he had "leapt" into. successfully correcting the past, Sam continues to leap randomly to another place and time within the second half of the 20th century, putting things right that once went wrong and hoping each time that his next leap will be the leap home. (Cerone, Daniel, 1990).

When we should apply automation

Scouring the internet for information about automation I have found two rulles which lies close to my hearth, and I would like to add one from my own believes

  • 1. The first rule of any technology used in a business is that automation applied to an efficient operation will magnify the efficiency. (Bill Gates)
  • 2. The second rule is that automation applied to an inefficient operation will magnify the inefficiency. (Bill Gates)
  • 3. The third rule is that automation applied to operations which are greatly enjoyed by people will magnify drops in people motivation. (My Own)

Let us understand the three rules above. The first rule tells us that we can apply an automation processes to operations that are well proven and known to deliver business value. Spending time and effort into consolidating the operation with an automated process will only increase efficiency of the operation itself as automated tasks tend to be robust in terms of delivering the same level of service and quality.

The second rule tells us that if we apply an automation process to an operation which is already struggling to deliver business value and the level of the service is doubtful, the automation will only increase inefficiency as the customer is not recieving what he espected in the first place and the developing team has an extra burden needing to fix not only the operation but the automation as well.

The third rule which is my own tells us to keep business operations which cause joy among staff and users alike. Doing, a quantum leap from imagining

Short-term success is common, even if you know how to choose the right test automation tools for each role in your organization.

It's not that hard to write a little code to exercise a system. The problem comes 6 to 12 months later, when a test run takes hours, the test environment and results get flaky, and the programmers change the expected behavior of the system, transforming the nature of the tool from automation to change detection.

Success in automation is less about getting it right and more about avoiding mistakes that let costly defects get through—and even small bugs can have big consequences. Eventually, someone has to write the code. Even if the record/playback tool claims to be codeless, sooner or later your software will produce dates that need to be compared to today's date and formatted, and you'll need to drop down into some kind of code editor. The person writing the code is probably not a professional programmer, but even were that so, it is tempting to focus more on getting the code done than on doing it well.

Putting right what I made wrong

There is no right way to do automation, but there are certainly some wrong ones. "Quick wins" may be cheap and easy, but not when they result in a system that is expensive, or even impossible, to maintain. Worse, it could create extra delayed feedback loops between programmers and business operations.

Instead of jumping into driving the pipeline, take a step back. Look at the layers of the application. See what processes are actually repetitive and how much time you'd save by automating them.

Review your playbook for automation and pick something that could give you a big win for small effort. Engage the programmers to create a solution for the entire team. Mind the speed of feedback, focus on things that add speed but not drag, avoid the worst of the mistakes, and you'll probably be OK.
